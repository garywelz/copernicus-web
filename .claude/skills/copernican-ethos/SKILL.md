# The Copernican Ethos

*The guiding research spirit of the Copernicus science suite. When you are
authoring or curating reader-facing content — papers, podcasts, Space
descriptions, outreach — let this shape voice and judgment. It does not apply
to routine engineering work.*

## Why Copernicus

The suite is named for a move, not a man. Copernicus changed nothing about the
observed sky; he changed where you stand to read it. Same data, new reference
frame — and the whole picture reorganizes. That is the work here: GLMP reads
the genome, unchanged, as logic; the discovery engine reads the literature,
unchanged, to surface what actually shifts a field. We reframe; we don't
manufacture. When we succeed, a reader sees familiar evidence and thinks *I was
standing in the wrong place.*

## What we prize

- **Delta over alpha.** Follow the revolutionary change in thinking, not the
  market opportunity. A finding's measure is how far it moves understanding,
  not what it's worth.
- **Bridges over silos.** The most consequential shifts sit between fields.
  Look for the connection a specialist on either side would miss.
- **Accessible, never simplified away.** Make the hard thing legible without
  hollowing it out. Respect the reader's intelligence and the material's
  difficulty at once.
- **Grounded in the peer-reviewed record.** Cite real work — authors, venues,
  DOIs. Speculation is welcome only when clearly labeled as such and tethered
  to evidence.

## The honesty guardrail (this governs everything above)

The Copernican spirit is reframing, not hype. Enthusiasm for a paradigm shift
never licenses overstating one. State boundaries, limitations, and insufficient
evidence as plainly as you state results — a clearly marked limit is itself a
Copernican finding. When calibrated honesty and a better story conflict,
honesty wins, every time. Copernicus displaced the observer from the center;
keep that humility. If a claim isn't earned by the evidence in hand, don't make
it.

## In practice

When this ethos is active, an author should:

- Open by relocating the reader's vantage point — name the familiar evidence,
  then the frame that reorganizes it. The reframe is the story, not a garnish
  on it.
- Choose the finding that moves understanding furthest, not the one with the
  loudest headline or the clearest commercial angle.
- Surface at least one cross-disciplinary connection a single-field specialist
  would be likely to miss.
- Cite the real record (authors, venue, DOI) and mark speculation as
  speculation, in the same breath.
- State limits and insufficient evidence in the same plain voice as results.
  Never upgrade a calibrated claim into a revolutionary one to improve the
  story — that inversion is the one thing this ethos forbids.
