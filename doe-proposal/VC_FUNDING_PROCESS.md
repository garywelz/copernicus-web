# Thinking Through Venture Capital Funding

**Purpose:** Frame the VC process alongside grant funding (NSF, DOE). Different game: equity, milestones, and “why will this return 10x?” instead of technical merit and compliance.

---

## How VC Differs From Grants

| | **Grants (NSF, DOE SBIR)** | **Venture capital** |
|---|---------------------------|---------------------|
| **What they want** | Technical merit, feasibility, public benefit, compliance | **Return on investment** (10x+), large market, defensible position |
| **What you give** | Reports, deliverables, IP often retained by you | **Equity** (ownership %); investors have rights and often board/influence |
| **Decision** | Peer review, program officers, scoring | Partner vote; fit with fund stage, sector, and thesis |
| **Traction** | Prototype + plan often enough for Phase I | **Proof of demand** (users, revenue, LOIs) matters a lot; “idea + deck” is hard |
| **Stage** | Early R&D is the point | They want to see path to **scale** and **exit** (acquisition, IPO) |
| **Process** | FOA → submit → wait → award/decline | **Outreach → pitch → relationship → term sheet → due diligence → close** (often 2–6+ months) |

**Bottom line:** Grants fund *research and development*. VC funds *companies* they believe can grow fast and generate returns. Your job is to tell the story of a **business**, not only a research project.

---

## Typical VC Process (High Level)

1. **Prep** — Deck, one-pager, financials (even simple), data room. Know your numbers (users, revenue, pipeline, burn, runway).
2. **Target** — Identify funds that invest at your **stage** (pre-seed, seed, Series A) and **sector** (AI, research tools, B2B SaaS, verticals like academia/life sciences).
3. **Outreach** — Warm intro (preferred) or cold: email + deck or link. Apply to relevant accelerators if that fits.
4. **First meeting** — Short pitch + Q&A. They’re judging: problem size, solution, team, traction, fit with their thesis.
5. **Follow-on** — More meetings, deeper product/team/market. They may ask for metrics, customer calls, technical review.
6. **Term sheet** — Offer: amount, valuation (or cap/discount for convertible), key terms (board, liquidation preference, etc.). You negotiate or walk.
7. **Due diligence** — Legal, cap table, contracts, IP, financials. They confirm what you said.
8. **Close** — Sign, wire, announce (if desired).

**Timeline:** From first meeting to wire can be 2–6+ months. Multiple no’s are normal; it’s a numbers and fit game.

## What You Already Have (From the Proposal) That Translates to VC

- **Problem:** Researchers drowning in siloed tools; need orientation, synthesis, provenance. Clear and relatable.
- **Solution:** AI research briefings + structured metadata + knowledge graph + MCP/APIs. You have **live product** (copernicusai.fyi, Knowledge Engine, Hugging Face demos), not just slides.
- **Evidence:** Preprints, multiple working prototypes, deployed system. Strong for “technical credibility” and “can ship.”
- **Market strategy:** Subscription (individuals) → institutional licenses → API/partnerships. Fits a **B2B / B2B2C** narrative.
- **Commercialization path:** Already in the DOE narrative; for VC you sharpen **revenue model, pricing, and go-to-market**.

**Gaps to think through for VC:**

- **Traction in VC language:** Users (how many?), revenue (MRR/ARR?), waitlist, LOIs or letters from institutions, usage metrics (briefings generated, searches, etc.). Even early numbers help.
- **Market size:** TAM/SAM/SOM. “Researchers and knowledge workers” is big; narrow to “academic researchers in the US” or “R&D teams in pharma” etc., with a number (e.g. $X B).
- **Team:** Solo founder vs team. VC often wants “team that can execute”; how you frame your background (research + product + domain) and any advisors/contractors matters.
- **Ask:** How much, at what stage (pre-seed, seed), and **use of funds** (18–24 months of runway to hit what milestones: product, revenue, growth).
- **Why now / why you:** AI and LLMs make the product possible now; you have a head start with working system and research framing.

## Materials You’ll Need (VC “Data Package”)

1. **Pitch deck (10–15 slides)**  
   Problem, solution, product (demos/screenshots), market size, business model, traction, team, competition, roadmap, ask and use of funds.

2. **One-pager**  
   One page: problem, solution, traction, team, ask. For quick email and first touch.

3. **Financials**  
   Simple is fine at early stage: current burn, runway, projected revenue/costs for 18–24 months, assumptions (e.g. conversion, pricing).

4. **Data room (when they get serious)**  
   Cap table, incorporation docs, key contracts, IP summary, past/current grants (NSF, DOE status). Keeps things organized and speeds due diligence.

5. **Demo / live product**  
   You have this. Always be ready to show the best 3–5 minutes of the product.

## Who to Target (Stage and Sector)

- **Pre-seed / seed:** Small checks ($500K–$2M), often for “we have product and early traction, need to get to PMF or first revenue.” Fits “solo or tiny team, live product, not yet scaled.”
- **Sector:** AI, B2B SaaS, “future of work,” research tools, vertical SaaS (e.g. life sciences, academia). Some funds explicitly do “science / research / data” or “AI infrastructure.”
- **Where to look:**  
  - Funds that have invested in research tools, AI for professionals, or vertical SaaS.  
  - AngelList, Crunchbase, fund websites (“portfolio,” “thesis”).  
  - Accelerators (e.g. Y Combinator, Techstars, domain-specific) if you want a program + small check + network.

**NY / Northeast:** Some funds and angels focus on NY or Northeast; others are stage/sector-first regardless of geography.

## Practical Next Steps

1. **Lock the story on paper** — One-pager + outline of a 10–12 slide deck. Use the DOE narrative as the backbone; swap “intellectual merit” for “market and traction.”
2. **Write down traction** — Users, revenue, usage, pipeline, LOIs. Even “X briefings generated, Y returning users” is a start. Update as you go.
3. **Define the ask** — “Raising $X at pre-seed/seed to do Y over 18 months (product, first paid customers, institutional pilots).”
4. **List 20–30 investors** — By stage (pre-seed/seed) and sector (AI, research tools, B2B). Prioritize those with portfolio companies in adjacent spaces.
5. **Get warm intros** — Through advisors, network, LinkedIn, accelerators. Cold outreach works but conversion is lower.
6. **Practice the pitch** — 3 min, 5 min, 10 min versions. Emphasize problem → solution → product (demo) → traction → ask.

---

## How This Fits With Grants

- **Grants** = non-dilutive, milestone-driven, good for R&D and credibility. NSF/DOE can strengthen the “we’re serious and technical” story for VCs.
- **VC** = dilution, but capital to move faster (hire, sales, marketing) and scale. Many deep-tech and research-tool companies do **both**: grant for R&D, VC for commercialization and growth.
- You can say in the VC narrative: “We’re pursuing NSF/DOE for R&D; this round is to get to revenue and institutional pilots so we’re in a stronger position for Phase II and growth.”
